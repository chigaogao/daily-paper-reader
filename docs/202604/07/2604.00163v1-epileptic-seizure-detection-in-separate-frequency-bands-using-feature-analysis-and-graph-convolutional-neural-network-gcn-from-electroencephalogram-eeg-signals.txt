Abstract 

Epileptic seizures are neurological disorders characterized by abnormal and excessive electrical 

activity in the brain, leading to recurrent seizure events. Electroencephalogram (EEG) signals play 

a crucial role in seizure diagnosis by capturing both tempo ral and spatial dynamics of neural 

activity. Although recent deep learning approaches have achieved high seizure detection accuracy, 

they often lack interpretability and neurophysiological insight. This study focuses on ictal -phase 

EEG feature analysis for accurate and frequency -aware seizure detection. Raw EEG signals are 

decomposed into five frequency bands —Delta (0.5 –4 Hz), Theta (4 –8 Hz), Alpha (8 –13 Hz), 

Lower Beta (13 –22 Hz), and Higher Beta (22 –30 Hz). Eleven discriminative features are extracted 

fro m each band and modeled using a Graph Convolutional Neural Network (GCN), where EEG 

electrodes are represented as graph nodes to capture spatial dependencies across brain regions. 

Experiments conducted on the CHB -MIT scalp EEG dataset demonstrate detection accuracies of 97.1%, 97.13%, 99.5%, 99.7%, and 51.4% for the Delta, Theta, Alpha, Lower Beta, and Higher 

Beta bands, respectively, with an overall broadband accuracy of 99.01%. The results highlight the 

superior discriminative power of mid -frequency bands and reveal fre quency -specific seizure 

characteristics. The proposed band -specific GCN framework enhances spatial interpretability and 

diagnostic precision compared to conventional broadband EEG approaches. 

Keywords: Epilepsy, EEG, Seizure Detection, Graph Convolutional Network, Frequency Bands, 

Feature Extraction 

Science4Impact Statement 

This paper presents a new, multi -frequency band framework with Graph Convolutional Neural 

Networks (GCN) that can effectively detect epileptic seizures from the EEG signal. It combines 

high -end feature engineering with models of brain connectivity to provide high accuracy over the 

different frequency bands, which provides a clinically interpretable and therapeutically efficient 

approach . Innovation opens the door to frequency band -oriented analysis for seizure treatment ,

which has made a substantial contribution to the diagnosis and treatment of epilepsy. 

# 1.  Introduction 

The human brain is recognized as the most complex organ, consisting of approximately 86 billion 

neurons (Herculano -Houzel, 2009 ) that communicate with one another to form a complex network 

known as the nervous system. A healthy brain cell transmits a well -defined and stable pattern of 

electrical impulses ; however, when brain cells exhibit electrical dysfunction or excessive electrical 

discharges, it is considered a neurological disorder In the last twenty years, numerous research 

efforts have focused on neurological disorders including Epileptic seizures, Parkinson's disease, 

Alzheimer's, Autism, Dementia, Depression, and Schizophre nia, among others. Of these neurological conditions, epileptic seizures are particularly prevalent worldwide, impacting 

approximately 50 million people (World Health Organization ,2024 ) according to the World Health 

Organization (WHO ). An epileptic seizure is an abnormal brain condition characterized by 

recurrent seizures that originate from excessive and uncontrolled electrical activity in neuronal 

cells. Understanding the relationship between brain electrical patterns and seizures is essential for 

accurate diagnosis and treatment ( Gao et al., 2020 ). 

EEG signals are the most important non -invasive real -time recording method for brain electrical 

waves used to diagnose seizures (Wang et al., 2023 ). EEG testing is a painless, non -invasive 

treatment procedure that involves placing a combination of electrodes or montages on the scalp to 

investigate fluctuations in brain voltage, allowing clinicians to observe brain rhythms for both 

normal cognitive activities and abnormal occurrences (Wang et al.,2023 ). Frequency is a

significant component of the EEG signal, and assessing abnormal electrical wave patterns typically 

ranges from 0.01 to 100 Hz. The frequencies of the brain are divided into five main bands: alpha, 

beta , gamma, theta, and delta ( Figure 1 ). ( Table 1 ) shows the frequencies associated with each 

band.

Manually detecting seizures from EEG signals by a physician is a time -consuming and labor -

intensive task where there is a high degree of variation in person -to -person seizure signals. 

Therefore, by moving away from this manual system and using a computer -aided system, a 

neurologist can automatically detect seizure signals from EEG signals with minimal effort and 

quickly and with great reliability. The computer aided automated system provide quicker analysis, 

reduce diagnostic load, and enhance the consistency in EEG interpretation for seizure detection 

(Bomela et al., 2020 ). 

The most common automatic detection approaches rely on traditional machine learning algorithms 

combined with handcrafted features, such as wavelet coefficients, power spectral density (PSD), 

entropy, Lyapunov exponents, and Hjorth parameters (Wang et al., 2023 ). Hjorth parameters are 

essentially time -domain features that are particularly useful for analyzing nonlinear and 

nonstationary signal patterns (Alawee et al., 2023 ). On the other hand, the combination of time -

domain and frequency -domain features can improve classification performance (Singh et al., 2023) ;

nevertheless, feature extraction approaches remain limited in their ability to fully represent the 

nonlinear, nonstationary, and multidimensional characteristics of EEG signals. Furthermore, 

traditional machine learning algorithms fail to capture inter -channel spatial relationships, which 

are crucial for understanding seizure patterns from EEG signal propagation and synchronization 

(Jibon et al., 2023a; Jibon et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2 024 ). Fig. 1. EEG signals in different frequency bands. 

Deep learning is increasingly being considered more effective than machine learning in research 

fields that require large amounts of data (Shoeibi et al., 2022 ). Traditional machine learning 

methods use less training data than DL models. Deep learning (DL) method for automatic seizure 

detection involves two steps. This feature extraction uses spectral or temporal data from signals. 

The features can be analy zed statistically before being given to a classifier. This two -stage strategy 

yields excellent result s but requires advanced deep learning skills for preprocessing and extracting 

optimal features from raw data. CNNs, RNNs, and CNN -RNN networks are trained directly on 

raw or minimally processed EEG data, learning hierarchical spatial and temporal features without 

requiring manual feature extraction ( Xu, G. et al., 2020 ). These models are very robust and 

generalize well, although they have an inherent assumption of a Euclidean grid -like structure ( Fan, 

Z. et al., 2024 ). This presupposition fails to correspon d to the non -Euclidean geometry of the locations of EEG electrodes, which restricts their capacity to capture the non -Euclidean geometry 

of brain interconnectivity and the non -Euclidean interactions among different brain regions. 

To overcome such structural limitations, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), particularly Graph 

Convolutional Networks (GCNs), have become an effective analytical model for EEG -based 

seizure detection. In contrast to traditional ML and DL models, which assume that EEG channels 

can be used as independent variables or that channel data are arranged in a grid format, GCNs 

model EEG electrodes as nodes of a graph and represent channel functional or anatomica l

interactions as edges. This formulation aligns with the ph ysiological structure of the human brain, 

which consists of four principal lobes: frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital, each with specific 

neural functions. EEG electrodes positioned across these lobes record regional activity, and 

seizures tend to propagate along these anatomical connections. Modeling of EEG using GCNs can 

simultaneously learn locali zed patterns inside the lobes and long -range synchroni zation between 

lobes and therefore offer some degree of spatial intuition not attainable by ML and traditional DL 

architectures ( Wu, F. et al .). The graph -based view enables GCNs to model the evolution of 

seizures, the existence of inter -lobe connections, and propagation with clinical importance. This 

research presents a GCN -based epileptic seizure detection system, with a focus on accurate 

detection of the ictal phase based on preset seizure onset and offset labels. The proposed method, 

however, in contrast to the available approaches, is based on active duration of th e seizure as 

opposed to broad per -seizure windows ( Huang, J., et al., 2024 ). The active seizure onset period, 

known as the ictal phase seizure, provides more precise information about epileptic seizures, which 

is more suitable for model training and classification, rather than other phases like pre -ictal or post -

ictal (Zhao, S., et al., 2024 ). For epileptic seizure detection, analyzing EEG signals across distinct frequency bands of the 

human brain plays a crucial role because the power distribution of the signal is typically observed 

within a particular frequency band . The differences in signal power distribution observed in 

different phases of seizures, such as pre -ictal, ictal, or post -ictal, make a relationship with the 

frequency band that can be clearly observed in the spectrogram or h eatmap of the signal. By 

embedding this change or variation in frequency bands into graph -based deep learning, it is 

possible to accurately estimate both the spatial and temporal dynamics of the brain simultaneously 

(Saminu et al., 2021 ). In ( Figure 2 ) a Time -Frequency heatmap visually illustrates how the signal 

powers vary in different frequencies.

# 2.  Related Works 

Medical professionals use EEG waves as a valuable source of data to examine brain activity and 

diagnose patients with neurological conditions. Delta (f ≤ 4 Hz), Theta (4 Hz ≤ f ≤ 8 Hz), Alpha 

(8 Hz < f ≤ 13 Hz), Beta (13 Hz < f ≤ 30 Hz), and Gamma (30 Hz < f ≤ 40 Hz) are the various frequency components from EEG signals that are helpful for medical analysis. The above -

mentioned frequency bands correspond to various mental and physical processes associated with 

the brain. The EEG signals are difficult to de tect because they are time -varying and non -stationary. 

Feature extraction utilizes time -frequency techniques such as the discrete wavelet transform, 

wavelet packet decomposition, dual -tree complex wavelet transforms, and empirical mode 

decomposition, among others. Decomposition based on wavelets serves as a powerful approach 

for identifying transients, spikes, and epileptic seizures in EEG recordings. 

Recent developments in deep learning have presented promising mechanisms for separating 

epileptic and non -epileptic EEG activity. In 2022, Tuncer et al. proposed a Bi -LSTM -based model, 

which was optimized with 10 neurons by systematic hyperparameter optimi zation. The major 

parameters investigated were optimizer type, learning rate, and the number of neurons, which gave 

the optimal architecture as 99% accuracy in binary classification and an average of 97.78% over 

all the evaluations. This plan not only indi cates how effective the Bi -LSTM models are but also 

reveals the large influence that hyperparameter optimization has on the performance of 

classification and the efficiency of the computations ( Tuncer, E. et al., 2022 ). 

Deep learning techniques have gained significant grip in medical diagnosis, especially for seizure 

detection from EEG signals. In this domain, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) made the 

most interest, often requiring EEG signals to be preprocessed into two -dimensional image formats 

for input. In 2020 and later in 2024 ( Kaziha, O. and Bonny, T., 2020 ) and ( Zhao, S., et al., 2024 )

applied CNNs to extract features in raw EEG data. CNNs have, however, difficulties in capturing 

the temporal dependencies, resu lting in a loss of information with time. To overcome this 

restriction, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) have been used as a more detailed analyzer of the 

sequential character of EEG waveforms. The LSTM networks have been mainly applied in handling the data of epileptic EEG, and they 

have integrated various time -related characteristics to advance the identification of seizures before 

classification. Though LSTMs are quite efficient at eliminating the gradient explosion issue of 

classic RNNs, they are not applicable in multidirectional time series modeling. To solve this 

limitation in 2025, ( Chekhmane, G. and Benali, R., 2025 ) suggested a Bidirectional LSTM (Bi -

LSTM), which applies the data process ing operations both forward and backward, so it is 

especially applicable to analyze long EEG tracks. Another example of this method can be seen 

where ( Geng, M., et al., 2020 ) used Bi -LSTM and S -transform in conjunction to identify seizures. 

Raw EEG segments are sized by S -transform to create time -frequency matrices, which are 

subdivided into blocks and passed to the Bi -LSTM to extract features and classify them. 

Subsequently, the post -processing, such as moving average filtering, threshold assessment, 

multi channel fusion, and collar methodology, is applied to optimize the performance of the 

detection, attaining 98.69% specificity and 98.09% sensitivity. 

Although the recurrent architecture, such as LSTM, Bi -LSTM, is excellent at temporal modeling, 

Graph -based methods have emerged as a robust approach for representing and analyzing complex 

relationships in EEG signal classification. In 2022, ( Grattarola, D., et al., 2022 ) used a Generalized 

Neural Network (GNN) to determine which parts of the brain were associated with individual 

electrodes during the interictal and ictal phases. Critical sites were detected by the GNN's attention -

based layer without any pri or knowledge of the seizure initiation zone. To mine larger data sets 

and investigate possible correlations between signals, (Chen et al.,2020 ) developed the EGCN 

model, a graph convolutional network. Graph Attention Network (GAT) and Bi -directional LSTM 

(BiLSTM) were used to create a hybrid framework for seizure detectio n, where GAT was used as 

the front end for spatial feature extraction and the BiLSTM network was utilized to explore time relations as the back end (He, J., et al., 2022 ). In 2023, (Wang et al. 2023) proposed the Sparse 

Spectra Graph Convolutional Network (SSGCNet), a lightweight and efficient model for epileptic 

seizure detection . This paper introduced a Weighted Neighborhood Field Graph (WNFG) 

representation that significantly reduces redundant connections and processing costs for existing 

graph -based approaches . The framework performs well in both time -domain and frequency -

domain EEG analysis. (Yang et al., 2024 ) proposed a dynamic graph neural network that uses an 

attention mech anism for single -channel EEG input , where classification is done by graph 

construction through empirical mode decomposition (EMD), and also optimizes the model through 

an optimal adjacency matrix. 

In 2023, ( Zheng et al., 2023 ) introduced a new method using knowledge distillation (KD) to 

personalize seizure detection while reducing the number of required EEG channels. They 

transferred learned by a pre -trained model to a lightweight validation model, allowing global and 

individualized seizure detection using the Temple University Hospital EEG Seizure Data Corpus 

(TUSZ). The system demonstrated competitive accuracy with as few as two EEG channels, 

demonstrating potential for wearable devices and real -wo rld use. The study emphasizes the 

significance of integrating patient -specific adjustments with pre -trained generalized knowledge. 

Subsequently, within the next year, ( Shen et al., 2024 ) suggested a real -time system for the 

detection of seizures based on the STFT transform. The proposed system transforms the EEG 

signal into the time -frequency representations and classifies them via a 29 -layer Google -Net CNN. 

The system analyzes continuous EEG signals via a 1.35 -second window with overlap and achieves 

remarkable results with 97.74% accuracy, 98.90% sensitivity, and a FP rate of 1 .94% at a 

processing time of 0.02 seconds per 2 seconds of the episode. By representing the raw EEG signals 

tran sformed into image -based representations , the system overcomes the need to perform extensive feature engineering and enables the CNN to automatically extract the discriminative 

features directly from the data. In a 2020 study, ( Bomela et al., 2020 ) adopted a dynamic learning 

strategy and considered the epileptic brain to be a time -evolving graph. The proposed framework 

employs two complementary methods where the Fourier Transform (FT) approach detects seizures 

by studying synchronization patterns in EEG channels, with a sensitivity of 93.6% and a false 

positive rate of 0.16 per hour. Lat er in 2024, ( Jibon et al., 2024 ) proposed a hybrid deep learning 

framework for epileptic seizure detection from EEG signals combining a Sequential Graph 

Convolutional Network (SGCN) and a Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DeepRNN). The SGCN 

localizes spatial features of seizure in the human brain , and the DeepRNN effectively identifies 

temporal dependencies of seizure signal s using GRU -based units, solving the vanishing gradient 

problem . Th is framework achieved 99.007% accuracy on the CHB -MIT dataset and 98.08 %

accuracy on the TUH dataset. Notably, one of the authors of this study has also contributed to the 

current work, which builds upon this approach to address extraction methods to reduce 

computational complexity and enhance detection accuracy .

# 3.  Proposed Methodology 

In this study, frequency band -specific seizure detection was performed through frequency band -

oriented analysis rather than conventional broadband EEG signal analysis. Comparisons are also 

shown with seizure detection performed on conventional broadband EEG signals. At the beginning 

of this work, the original EEG signal was separated into five well -defined frequency bands (delta, 

theta, alpha, lower beta, and higher beta bands) , and the timings of different seizure phases (pre -

ictal , ictal , post -ictal , etc.) were split according to the dataset. The fundamental aspect of this study 

is that 11 significant signal features were ext racted by analyzing only ictal -phase seizures to ensure 

the accuracy, precision, and credibility of seizure detection in this model . This model of frequency band -specific seizure detection has been learned through a graph convolutional network with 11 

signal features of the ictal phase, which simultaneously combines the spatial and temporal 

dynamics of seizure detection. The complete workflow of the proposed methodology is shown in 

Fig. 3 .

During the feature extraction s tage , the detailed proc edure of extracting 11 signal features from 

ictal phase seizures is explained . These 11 extracted features from the ictal phase seizure signal 

were trained by using a graph convolutional neural network (GCN). This creates a credible ,

interconnected graph -based model for frequency band -specific seizure detection activity. Graph -

based deep learning approach for seizure detection, ensuring minimal signal loss and consistent 

with the spatio -temporal nature of brain signal dynamics. The follow ing sections present a detailed 

description of the stages involved in the proposed approach.

comprehensively show seizure detection . The GCN model learns the spatiotemporal correlations 

within each band & smoothly combines spatial and temporal data. The proposed deep learning 

confirms the spatial nature of the connections across various brain regions and is consistent with 

the temporal signal dynamics . This is often critical for the identification of seizures. 

Aside from recording local seizure activity. The confusion matrices (Fig. 08 ) indicate that the 

model achieves stable accuracy across different frequency bands, suggesting reliable 

discrimination between seizure -related and non -seizure EEG activity , especially when these three 

methods are combined to utilize the broadband of frequencies, especially the Theta Band. 

Moreover, inter -patient variability is still a major impediment to seizure detection because 

everyone’s unique brain dynamics. While the model performed well within the tested datasets, its 

performance on datasets with higher noise levels or artifact contamination remains to be explored. 

# 8.  Conclusion 

In this study, the application of Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) to seizure detection 

enables the exploration of the brain's spatiotemporal characteristics while minimizing signal loss 

common in graph -based deep learning models. The proposed model for i ndependent seizure detection across five frequency bands (delta, theta, alpha, lower beta, higher beta) with improved 

accuracy per band, identifies frequency -specific seizure patterns and enhances the diagnostic 

precision over conventional broadband approaches. The combination of the consideration of high -

level feature extraction methods of specific frequency bands enabled us to improve the 

representation of EEG signals in a non -Euclidean feature space. Additionally, the use of predefined 

ictal -phase seizure features during m odel training enabled the proposed model to focus on 

clinically important features , resulting in improved sensitivity and lower false positive rates, with 

a key innovation being the use of a predefined set of seizure times from the CHB -MIT dataset. It 

improves the accuracy of seizure detection and still has a lightweight model architecture, which 

opens the way to real -life use in real -time and resource -constrained systems. It was experimentally 

demonstrated that the proposed model yields 0.967 F1 -score, 99. 025% specificity, 98.6% 

sensitivity, and 99% detection accuracy . A significant contribution of this research is that the 

proposed model demonstrates robust performance by identifying the preictal, ictal, and postictal 

phases as seizure activity across five frequency bands. This model for the detection of seizures can 

be used effectively within the specified bands, resulting in a clearer definition of the phases 

involved in seizures, which include pre -ictal, ictal, inter -ictal, and post -ictal phases, depending on 

the frequenc y bands involved. It’s worth noting that specific areas within the brain, which relate 

to where seizures originate, can greatly contribute to improving the accuracy with which seizures 

can be identified and targeted.
