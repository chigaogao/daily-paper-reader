# Subject-Aware Multi-Granularity Alignment for 

# Zero-Shot EEG-to-Image Retrieval 

Lin Jiang, Qingshan She, Jiale Xu, Haiqi Xu, Duanpo Wu , Zhenzhong Kuang 

 Abstract —Zero-shot EEG-to-image retrieval aims to 

decode perceived visual content from 

electroencephalography (EEG) by aligning neural 

responses with pretrained visual representations, providing 

a promising route toward scalable visual neural decoding 

and practical brain-computer interfaces. However, robust 

EEG-to-image retrieval remains challenging, because prior 

methods usually rely on either a single fixed visual target or 

a subject-invariant target construction scheme. Such 

designs overlook two important properties of visually 

evoked EEG signals: they preserve information across 

multiple representational scales, and the visual granularity 

best matched to EEG may vary across subjects. To address 

th ese issues, subject-aware multi-granularity alignment 

(SAMGA) framework is proposed for zero-shot EEG-to-

image retrieval. SAMGA first constructs a subject-aware 

visual supervision target by adaptively aggregating multiple 

intermediate representations from a pretrained vision 

encoder, allowing the model to absorb subject-dependent 

granularity deviations during training while preserving 

subject-agnostic inference. Building on this adaptive target 

construction, a coarse-to-fine cross-modal alignment 

strategy is further designed with a shared encoder wherein 

the coarse stage stabilizes the shared semantic geometry 

and reduces subject-induced distribution shift, and the fine 

stage further improves instance-level retrieval 

discrimination. Extensive experiments on the THINGS-EEG 

benchmark demonstrate that the proposed method 

achieves 91.3% Top-1 and 98.8% Top-5 accuracy in the 

intra-subject setting, and 34.4% Top-1 and 64.8% Top-5 

accuracy in the inter-subject setting, outperforming recent 

state-of-the-art methods. These results indicate that robust 

EEG visual decoding depends not only on stronger neural 

encoders, but also on how the visual supervision target is 

constructed and adapted to the multi-scale, subject-variable 

nature of EEG signals.

I. INTRODUCTION 

NDERSTANDING how the brain represents visual 

information is a long-standing goal in neuroscience 

and artificial intelligence. Visual neural decoding, 

which aims to infer perceived visual content from neural 

activity, is important both for probing the mechanisms of 

human perception and for enabling practical brain-computer 

interfaces (BCIs) in assistive communication and intelligent 

interaction [1] -[3] . With the rapid development of large-scale 

representation learning, this problem has increasingly been 

formulated as a cross-modal alignment task, where neural 

responses are projected into a semantic space and matched 

against visual representations through similarity-based 

retrieval [4] -[8] . This formulation naturally enables zero-shot 

decoding by leveraging pretrained visual spaces as 

transferable semantic anchors for unseen categories. 

Electroencephalography (EEG) is a particularly attractive 

noninvasive modality for visual decoding, offering high 

temporal resolution, portability, and low -cost relative to 

alternatives like fMRI and MEG [3] , [9] , [10] . However, EEG -

based visual decoding remains highly challenging. The signals 

are inherently noisy, spatially coarse, and vulnerable to inter-

subject variability, trial-wise fluctuations, and artifacts, making 

it difficult to extract stable semantic information from transient 

neural responses [9] -[11] . This difficulty is significantly 

amplified in zero-shot settings, which require models to 

generalize to unseen classes while maintaining robustness 

across different subjects [5] , [7] , [12] .

The convergence of large-scale benchmarks and pretrained 

vision-language models has catalyzed significant advancements 

in EEG-based visual decoding. Notably, THINGS-EEG is a

large-vocabulary, rapid serial visual presentation (RSVP) 

benchmark for EEG-to-image retrieval , which has emerged as 

the standard testbed for zero-shot neural decoding [5] , [12] .

Leveraging these resources, contrastive learning has become the 

dominant paradigm for aligning EEG features with pretrained 

visual embeddings [4] , [6] , [7] . Pioneering works like NICE 

established the feasibility of self-supervised EEG-image 

alignment for zero-shot retrieval, while its successor, NICE++, 

enhanced semantic consistency through language-guided 

contrastive learning [4] , [6] . Building on this foundation, 

contemporary studies have introduced interpretable alignment 

mechanisms, uncertainty-aware visual priors, cognitively 

inspired augmentations, and shared semantic projections to

progressively bridge the modality gap between brain signals and 

visual representations [7] , [8] , [13] .

Despite this progress, robust EEG-to-image retrieval 

remains challenging, especially in cross-subject settings. A key 

limitation of existing methods is tha t they usually align EEG 

features with either a single visual target, as in NICE/NICE++, 

or a fixed aggregation of multiple views [4] , [6] as in 

NeuroBridge, while UBP also still performs alignment against 

pretrained visual targets under a fixed matching paradigm [8] ,

[13] . Such designs implicitly assume that the visual granularity 

best matched to EEG is largely shared across subjects. However, 

prior studies have shown that EEG signals are inherently noisy, 

exhibit substantial inter-subject variability [10] , and preserve 

information across multiple representational scales rather than at 

a single level of abstraction [6] , [8] , [14] . Consequently, forcing 

all subjects to align with the same fixed visual target can leave a

residual mismatch that becomes more pronounced under subject 

shift. Recent work on granularity mismatch further shows that 

directly aligning neural signals to highly abstract final-layer 

visual embeddings can be suboptimal , and that intermediate 

visual representations often provide a better match to the multi-

scale nature of neural signals [14] . Nevertheless, even 

intermediate-layer alignment is still commonly treated as 

subject-invariant and shallow alignment itself still relies on 

layer-wise search to identify the best alignment target, leaving 

the subject-dependent aspect of target granularity insufficiently 

addressed [14] .

To tackle the above problems, a novel subject-aware multi-

granularity alignment framework (SAMGA) is proposed for 

EEG-to-image retrieval. SAMGA constructs a subject-aware 

multi-granularity target by aggregating multiple intermediate 

visual representations, allowing the model to better 

accommodate subject-dependent granularity differences. 

Motivated by the granularity mismatch identified in shallow 

alignment that intermediate representations provide a more 

suitable granularity match for neural signals than the final layer 

embeddings of vision backbones, while also going beyond 

existing intermediate layer methods by explicitly modeling the 

subject-dependent nature of target granularity [14] . During 

training, a subject-aware routing mechanism is introduced to 

absorb subject-specific granularity deviations and to learn a

more flexible target that better matches the multi-scale 

information preserved in EEG signals. During inference, only 

the global routing weights are used. Building on this adaptive 

target construction, we further develop a coarse -to -fine cross-

modal alignment strategy with a shared encoder. The coarse 

stage first stabilizes the shared semantic geometry and reduces 

subject-induced distribution shift, while the fine stage further 

enhances instance level discriminability for retrieval. In this 

way, SAMGA jointly improves target adaptability and 

alignment robustness, leading to more reliable EEG-to-image 

retrieval under both intra-subject and inter-subject settings. The 

main contributions are summarized as follows. 

1) A unified framework named SAMGA is proposed for 

zero-shot EEG-to-image concept retrieval. The 

framework explicitly addresses cross-subject decoding 

from the perspective of subject-dependent granularity 

mismatch and improves alignment robustness. 

2) A subject-aware multi-granularity target construction 

module is designed to adaptively aggregate multiple 

visual representations to form a more flexible visual 

target. By covering complementary granularity cues, 

the design provides a better match to the multi-scale 

information preserved in EEG signals. 

3) A coarse-to-fine cross-modal alignment strategy with a

shared encoder is applied to encourage stable shared-

space formation under subject shift, whil e the fine 

stage further improves instance-level discriminability 

for retrieval. 

II. RELATED WORK 

A. EEG-to-Image Decoding in Pretrained Semantic 

Spaces 

Visual neural decoding aims to infer information about 

perceived visual stimuli from patterns of brain activity, 

typically by learning mappings between neural response 

patterns and stimulus or condition labels [1] , [2] . Recent EEG-

based studies have increasingly formulated this task as cross-

modal retrieval within pretrained semantic spaces, where 

individual EEG trials are projected into a shared embedding 

space and decoded via similarity matching against candidate 

image representations [4] , [6] -[8] . Large-vocabulary 

benchmarks, such as THINGS-EEG, have played a pivotal 

role in establishing this paradigm by enabling 200-way zero-

shot concept retrieval that extends beyond traditional closed-

set classification [5] . Meanwhile, newer benchmarks like 

CrossPT-EEG further emphasize evaluation under cross-

participant and cross-time generalization [12] . Within this 

context, pretrained vision-language models provide a

transferable organization of visual concepts and have emerged 

as the dominant image-side semantic anchors for EEG-to-

image retrieval [8] , [15] .

Pioneering this approach, NICE demonstrated that self-

supervised contrastive alignment between EEG and image 

embeddings can support robust zero-shot retrieval, effectively 

reframing EEG visual decoding as a representation alignment 

problem rather than a purely supervised recognition task [4] .

BraVL built upon this foundation by incorporating linguistic 

supervision into a brain-visual-language framework to 

improve semantic transfer under limited paired data [3] , while 

NICE++ further strengthened semantic consistency through 

language-guided contrastive learning [6] . Subsequent studies 

have similarly explored more structured cross-modal 

alignment strategies, including interpretable alignment 

mechanisms [7] , semantic decoupling for improved visual-

EEG consistency [16] , and multimodal semantic bridging via 

contrastive representation learning [17] .

Recent work has further broadened this retrieval-based 

paradigm toward image reconstruction and target refinement. 

For instance, ATM projects EEG signals into a CLIP-aligned 

semantic subspace, supporting both retrieval and 

reconstruction within a unified pipeline [18] , while 3

·

DreamDiffusion couples aligned EEG embeddings with 

diffusion-based generation to enhance EEG-driven image 

synthesis [19] . Concurrently, several methods have begun to 

question the use of a fixed, final-layer visual target. UBP 

reduces the vision-brain mismatch by suppressing fine-grained 

visual details that are difficult to recover from noninvasive 

neural signals [8] . Similarly, NeuroBridge enhances 

robustness through cognitive prior augmentation and shared 

semantic projection [13] , and Shallow Alignment 

demonstrates that intermediate visual representations can 

better match the granularity preserved in neural signals than 

highly compressed final-layer embeddings [14] . More recently, 

NeuroCLIP has explored prompt-based adaptation of the 

pretrained CLIP space itself, rather than treating it as a static 

target [20] . Taken together, these studies begin to shift 

attention from encoder design alone to the construction of the 

visual supervision target. 

In parallel, representation-first EEG foundation models 

suggest that large-scale self-supervised pretraining can 

improve the stability and transferability of neural 

representations prior to cross-modal alignment [21] , [22] .

Nevertheless, most existing EEG-to-image decoding methods 

still rely on a largely fixed semantic target and alignment 

paradigm, which remains insufficiently adaptive to the noisy, 

multi-scale, and subject-variable nature of EEG signals. This 

limitation motivates the need for a more adaptive EEG-visual 

alignment framework. 

B. Visual Target Construction and Granularity Matching 

for EEG-Visual Alignment 

An ongoing challenge in EEG-to-image decoding is that 

alignment quality depends not only on the EEG encoder, but 

also on how the visual target is constructed. Earlier neural 

decoding studies have established that brain activity can be 

mapped to hierarchical visual features rather than being 

restricted merely to class labels or raw pixels [23] , [24] . For 

example, Horikawa and Kamitani [23] demonstrated that 

arbitrary seen and imagined object categories can be decoded 

by predicting multi-level deep visual features from brain 

activity, while Shen et al. [24] further showed that image 

reconstruction can be substantially improved by jointly 

exploiting multiple levels of visual representations. These 

findings suggest that target representations at different depths 

preserve distinct mixtures of structural and semantic 

information, and are therefore not interchangeable for neural 

decoding. 

Recent studies further underscore the pivotal role of the 

target space itself in neural decoding . MindEye organizes 

brain decoding around a pretrained multimodal latent space 

and separates retrieval-oriented alignment from 

reconstruction-oriented generation, revealing that the 

geometry of the target space can directly affect semantic 

retrieval and reconstruction fidelity [25] . Fang et al. [26] 

explicitly addressed the semantic gap in generalized fMRI-to-

image reconstruction, suggesting that stable decoding depends 

not only on the brain-to-feature mapping, but also on how the 

target semantic space is structured. Similarly, MinD-Vis 

combines sparse masked brain modeling with double-

conditioned latent diffusion, indicating that semantically 

consistent decoding benefits from coupling neural 

representation learning with carefully designed target-side 

conditioning [27] . Collectively, these studies indicate that 

visual targets should often be adapted, regularized, or 

reparameterized for neural decoding, rather than treated as 

static final-layer supervision. 

A complementary line of work attempts to refine target 

semantics more explicitly. BrainSem [28] learns a joint 

semantic latent space between brain signals and machine 

representations to improve cross-modal consistency, while 

Mind Artist [29] further combines semantic modeling with 

optimal transport and representation decoupling to enhance 

semantic fidelity under limited supervision. Benchetrit et al. 

[30] also showed that decoding performance depends strongly 

on the choice of pretrained visual embedding family and 

representational level, suggesting that target granularity itself 

is a critical design variable rather than a fixed choice. In 

summary, existing studies indicate that robust EEG-visual 

alignment depends not only on stronger neural representations, 

but also on how the visual target is organized, selected, and 

calibrated. However, most current methods still rely on a fixed 

target transformation or a globally chosen representation level, 

thereby offering limited flexibility to adapt target granularity 

to subject-dependent EEG variability. To address this 

limitation, our method constructs a subject-aware multi-

granularity visual target rather than using a globally fixed 

target representation. 

C. Cross-Subject Robust Alignment in Shared 

Embedding Spaces 

A critical bottleneck in EEG-to-image retrieval is that 

cross-modal alignment must remain stable under subject shift. 

Pioneering studies on brain decoding have shown that cross-

subject transfer becomes more feasible when neural responses 

are organized in a common representational space rather than 

being treated as isolated subject-specific patterns. Haxby et al. 

[31] demonstrated that fine-grained visual representations can 

be embedded into a common high-dimensional space shared 

across individuals, while Ho et al. [32] further illustrated that 

hierarchical visual information can be preserved across 

subjects through explicit neural code conversion for cross-

subject decoding and reconstruction. These findings imply that 

robust generalization depends not only on stronger subject-

wise mappings, but also on whether a stable shared 

representational geometry can be formed across individuals. 

Recent visual brain decoding studies further substantiate 

this perspective. Notably, MindEye2 maps multiple subjects 

into a shared-subject latent space before decoding into CLIP-

aligned image representations, revealing that shared-subject 

modeling can substantially improve out-of-subject 

generalization while reducing the amount of subject-specific 

training data [33] . MindBridge extends this direction through a

unified cross-subject decoding framework and subject-

invariant representation learning, demonstrating that a single 

model can support multiple subjects while remaining 4

·

competitive with subject-specific pipelines [34] . These studies 

consistently indicate that cross-subject transfer benefits from 

first stabilizing the shared latent structure prior to performing 

downstream retrieval or reconstruction within that space. 

A related line of inquiry focuses on optimizing how new 

subjects are brought into the shared space. MindTuner 

integrates multi-subject pretraining with lightweight subject 

adaptation and semantic correction to improve cross-subject 

visual decoding under scarce data [35] . MindAligner further 

introduces explicit brain functional alignment and a brain 

transfer matrix to project new subjects into a reusable 

decoding space [36]. Similarly, MindLink emphasizes subject-

agnostic cross-subject decoding through unified modeling 

across individuals [37] . Overall, prior studies indicate that 

robust cross-subject visual decoding depends not only on 

better subject-specific fitting, but also on how a shared space 

is constructed, aligned, and adapted across individuals. 

However, most current methods still emphasize cross-subject 

alignment at a single stage, or treat shared-space formation 

and fine-grained cross-modal discrimination as a single 

coupled objective, leaving limited room to progressively 

stabilize global geometry and then refine retrieval 

discrimination. To address this limitation, our method adopts a

coarse-to-fine cross-modal alignment strategy with a shared 

encoder.

B. Coarse-to-Fine Cross Modal Alignment with a Shared 

Encoder

challenge is to learn a shared embedding space that is both 

stable across subjects and discriminative for retrieval. For 

EEG signals, this is nontrivial because subject variability and 

signal noise can easily disturb cross-modal geometry during 

training. If instance-level retrieval discrimination is 

emphasized before a consistent shared space is formed, the 

model may overfit pairwise matching patterns and suffer from 

unstable transfer across subjects. To address this issue, we 

introduce a shared encoder to impose a common 

transformation on EEG and image representations, and further 

optimize the model in a coarse-to-fine manner. The coarse 

stage first stabilizes the global semantic geometry and reduces 

cross-modal discrepancy, and the fine stage then refines 6

·

instance-level discrimination on top of the stabilized shared 

space. By explicitly separating shared-space formation from 

retrieval refinement, this design leads to more robust 

optimization and better cross-subject generalization.

IV. EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS 

A. Datasets and Preprocessing 

We evaluate the proposed method on two large-scale 

neural decoding benchmarks built on object images from the 

THINGS stimulus space, namely THINGS-EEG and 

THINGS-MEG [5] , [38] . THINGS-EEG contains EEG 

recordings from 10 subjects collected under a rapid serial 

visual presentation (RSVP) paradigm. Following the standard 

zero-shot retrieval protocol used in prior work, the training 

split contains 1,654 concepts, each associated with 10 images 

and 4 repetitions per image, while the test split contains 200 

unseen concepts, each represented by one image repeated 80 

times. The RSVP stream uses a 200 ms stimulus onset 

asynchrony, consisting of 100 ms stimulus presentation 

followed by a 100 ms blank screen. We further evaluate on 

THINGS-MEG, which contains MEG recordings from 4

subjects performing the same visual object recognition task. 

Its training split comprises 1,854 concepts, each paired with 

12 distinct images and one trial per image, whereas the test 

split contains 200 unseen concepts, each represented by one 

image repeated 12 times. To ensure zero-shot evaluation, the 

200 test concepts are excluded from training. In the original 

MEG acquisition, each trial includes a 500 ms stimulus 

presentation followed by a 1000 ± 200 ms inter-stimulus 

interval. 

For fair comparison, we follow the same preprocessing 

protocol as Song et al . [4] . For THINGS-EEG, the signals are 

band-pass filtered to 0.1 –100 Hz, segmented into epochs from 

0 to 1000 ms relative to stimulus onset, baseline-corrected 

using the mean signal from the 200 ms pre-stimulus interval, 

and downsampled to 250 Hz. Multivariate noise normalization 

(MVNN) is then applied, and repetitions of the same image 

are averaged to improve the signal-to-noise ratio. For 

THINGS-MEG, signals from 271 sensors are processed using 

the same 0–1000 ms epoch window and 0.1 –100 Hz band-pass 

filter, followed by baseline correction with the 200 ms pre-

stimulus window and downsampling to 200 Hz. Repeated test 

trials are averaged, and channel-wise z-score normalization is 

applied across trials .

Fig .5 visualizes subject-specific deviations from the learned 

global visual-depth preference. Importantly, this figure does not 

show absolute routing weights; instead, each entry measures how

corresponding global routing weight at the same visual layer. 9

Positive values indicate that a subject assigns more weight to that

A shared pattern can be clearly observed across subjects. 

Most subjects assign lower-than-global weights to layer 24, 

while allocating relatively higher weights to layers 32 and 36. 

This indicates that, relative to the global baseline, deeper 

intermediate layers tend to contribute more strongly than the 

shallower intermediate layer. At the same time, the redistribution 

pattern is not identical across subjects. Some subjects, such as 

sub-01 and sub-09, retain positive deviations at layer 28, whereas 

others, such as sub-03 and sub-07, shift more strongly toward 

layers 32 and 36. Therefore, this figure should be interpreted as 

evidence that visual-depth preference is globally consistent but 

not strictly identical across subjects: the model learns a shared 

preference for intermediate visual layers together with stable 

subject-dependent refinements around that shared trend.

V. DISCUSSION 

The results of this study suggest that robust zero-shot 

EEG-to-image retrieval depends not only on the design of the 

EEG encoder, but also on how the visual supervision target is 

constructed. Recent EEG visual decoding methods have 

largely improved cross-modal alignment through stronger 

encoders, semantic projectors, or prior-guided training 

objectives. However, many of them still rely on a single fixed 10 

·

visual target or a globally shared target granularity. This 

assumption is restrictive for EEG-based retrieval, because 

visually evoked EEG signals are noisy, preserve information 

across multiple representational scales, and exhibit substantial 

inter-subject variability. In this context, the proposed SAMGA 

framework provides a different perspective by explicitly 

addressing subject-dependent granularity mismatch on the 

target side of cross-modal alignment. 

A key implication of our findings is that the visual depth 

best aligned with EEG should not be treated as universally 

fixed. Recent studies have shown that intermediate visual 

representations can provide a better granularity match for 

neural signals than highly compressed final-layer embeddings, 

because noninvasive neural recordings preserve a mixture of 

structural and semantic cues rather than purely abstract 

category-level information. Our results are consistent with this 

view, but further indicate that even intermediate-layer 

supervision should not be assumed to be fully subject-

invariant. By introducing subject-aware routing during 

training and reverting to a global routing prior during 

inference, SAMGA balances two competing goals: learning a

flexible target that can absorb subject-specific deviations, 

while still preserving generalization to unseen subjects without 

requiring subject identity at test time. 

The experimental and analytical results provide additional 

support for this interpretation. The improvements observed 

under both intra-subject and inter-subject settings suggest that 

adaptive target construction benefits not only within-subject 

retrieval, but also cross-subject generalization, where residual 

mismatch is often amplified. Moreover, the routing analysis 

indicates that visual-depth preference exhibits a shared global 

trend with limited but stable subject-dependent deviations, 

rather than being either completely fixed or entirely 

idiosyncratic. This helps explain why fixed-target methods 

may remain suboptimal under subject shift, and why learning 

a flexible but regularized target is beneficial for EEG visual 

decoding. 

Another important observation is that the proposed coarse-

to-fine alignment strategy appears to complement the target-

side adaptation. Prior work has shown that retrieval 

performance can be highly sensitive to the quality of the 

shared representation space, and that inappropriate alignment 

targets may weaken the informativeness of the supervision 

signal. In our framework, the coarse stage first stabilizes the 

global cross-modal geometry and reduces subject-induced 

distribution shift, whereas the fine stage then refines instance-

level discrimination within the stabilized shared space. This 

progressive optimization scheme is especially meaningful for 

EEG, where signal noise, trial fluctuation, and subject 

variability make direct end-to-end retrieval optimization more 

fragile. In this sense, SAMGA improves EEG-image retrieval 

not merely by adding another fusion mechanism, but by 

jointly coordinating target construction and alignment 

dynamics. 

Despite these advantages, the present work still has several 

limitations. First, the current routing mechanism models 

granularity adaptation at the subject level during training, but 

does not explicitly capture finer trial-wise or state-dependent 

variability. Second, although the current draft also outlines 

evaluation on THINGS-MEG, the main reported evidence in 

the present manuscript is still centered on THINGS-EEG, and 

fuller cross-modality validation would further strengthen the 

generality of the proposed framework. Third, the current 

model reveals category-wise differences in preferred visual 

depth, but does not yet explicitly incorporate category-aware 

or sample-adaptive routing into the target construction process. 

These limitations suggest several natural directions for future 

work, including sample-adaptive granularity selection, 

stronger uncertainty-aware routing, and unified target 

adaptation across subjects, categories, and recording sessions. 

VI .CONCLUSION 

In this work, we proposed Subject-Aware Multi-

Granularity Alignment (SAMGA) for zero-shot EEG-to-image 

retrieval. SAMGA addresses subject-dependent granularity 

mismatch by adaptively fusing multiple intermediate visual 

representations into a subject-aware visual target and then 

performing coarse-to-fine alignment in a shared embedding 

space. Experiments on THINGS-EEG showed that the 

proposed method achieved 91.3% Top-1 and 98.8% Top-5 

accuracy in the intra-subject setting, and 34.4% Top-1 and 

64.8% Top-5 accuracy in the inter-subject setting, 

outperforming recent baselines. These results indicate that 

robust EEG visual decoding depends not only on stronger 

neural representations, but also on target construction that 

better matches the multi-scale and subject-variable nature of 

EEG signals. Future work will explore finer-grained adaptive 

routing and broader validation across EEG/MEG settings.
