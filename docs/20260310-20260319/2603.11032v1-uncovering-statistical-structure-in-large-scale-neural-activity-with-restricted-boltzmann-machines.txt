(Dated: March 12, 2026) Large-scale electrophysiological recordings now allow simultaneous monitoring of thousands of neurons across multiple brain regions, revealing structured variability in neural population activ-ity. Understanding how these collective patterns emerge from microscopic neural interactions re-quires models that are scalable, predictive, and interpretable. Statistical physics provides principled frameworks to address this complexity, including maximum-entropy models that offer transparent descriptions of collective neural activity in small populations, but remain largely limited to pairwise interactions and modest system sizes. Here, we use Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBMs) to model the activity of ∼ 1500–2000 simultaneously recorded neurons from the Allen Institute Vi-sual Behavior Neuropixels dataset, spanning multiple cortical and subcortical regions of the mouse brain. RBMs are energy-based models that extend the maximum-entropy framework through la-tent variables, enabling the capture of higher-order dependencies while allowing explicit extraction of effective synaptic networks, including interactions beyond pairwise. Recent advances in efficient Markov Chain sampling and training enable accurate learning of these models at this scale. We show that RBMs reproduce the complex statistics of neural recordings with high accuracy. Generated samples match empirical pairwise and higher-order correlations, as well as global statistics such as the distribution of population activity. Beyond accurate data reconstruction, the inferred parame-ters provide direct access to effective interactions between neurons, revealing dominant coordination patterns in population activity. These couplings exhibit clear anatomical structure: neurons within visual cortical areas form coherent blocks of stronger interactions, consistent with shared engage-ment during visually driven behavior, whereas cross-area couplings are weaker and more diffuse. Furthermore, despite not being trained to reproduce temporal dependencies, Markov Chain Monte Carlo simulations of the model accurately reproduce the global neural relaxation dynamics. These results establish RBMs as scalable tools to extract interpretable statistical structure from large-scale neural recordings, linking collective neural activity to the organization of brain regions and task-related behavior. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Recent developments in large-scale electrophysiology, in particular the introduction of Neuropixels probes, have made it possible to record simultaneously from thou-sands of neurons distributed across multiple regions of the mouse brain [1]. These measurements reveal richly structured, high-dimensional population dynamics that are neither random nor strictly deterministic, but instead exhibit reproducible variability likely associated with the animal’s perceptual processes, internal states, and be-havioral outputs. A central challenge in contemporary

systems neuroscience is therefore to develop statistical frameworks that can faithfully represent, and ultimately interpret and elucidate the underlying mechanisms of, such collective neural activity. By analogy with equilibrium statistical mechanics, neural population activity can be regarded as a set of microscopic configurations of a high-dimensional system that would constitute a set of independent and identically distributed samples of an unknown high-dimensional dis-tribution. Within this perspective, the structure of the distribution would reflect latent, functionally relevant ef-fective interactions among neurons, and the primary ob-jective is to infer these interactions from experimental data. Maximum entropy approaches [2, 3] have been particularly successful in pursuing this goal over the past two decades. By constructing the least-structured distri-butions consistent with measured statistical constraints, 

> arXiv:2603.11032v1 [q-bio.NC] 11 Mar 2026

2they provide a principled and tractable framework for modeling neural populations as effective spin systems. In doing so, they characterize the probability landscape of population activity, linking collective dynamics to un-derlying interactions and revealing the statistical princi-ples governing large-scale neuronal networks, thereby en-abling quantitative connections between theoretical mod-els and experimental observations. Traditional approaches have aimed at identifying structured models that reproduce two central empiri-cal observables: mean firing rates and pairwise correla-tions between neurons. Operationally, this corresponds to solving the inverse Ising problem, i.e., inferring the parameters of an Ising spin-glass model whose statistics match the observed activity patterns. Such methods have been successfully applied to recordings of relatively small populations ( N ∼ 100) [3], yielding estimates of effec-tive synaptic couplings and accurately predicting higher-order statistics not explicitly imposed as constraints. However, this strategy becomes increasingly challenging in regimes relevant to modern large-scale recordings (in-cluding Neuropixels experiments [4, 5]), where the num-ber of simultaneously recorded neurons is large, while the recorded population still represents only a sparse subsam-ple of a much more extended circuit [6]. The number of parameters to infer scales rapidly with the square of the population size, quickly outpacing the amount of avail-able data and rendering reliable model fitting computa-tionally and statistically prohibitive. Within this con-text, recent efforts have focused on reducing the number of parameters by constraining the inferred model to a fixed interaction topology, such as tree-like graphs [7, 8] or planar structures [8]. Although these approaches en-able reliable inference in populations comprising thou-sands of neurons, they impose structural assumptions on the underlying interactions—for example, excluding feedback loops—and often require additional topological selection or tuning. Beyond the sheer number of parameters, limiting the model to pairwise interactions constitutes a substan-tial restriction, as such models are largely insensitive to genuine higher-order structure in population activity. Higher-order interactions have been argued to play an important role in reproducing empirical statistics [9–12], capturing collective phenomena such as synchronization [13, 14], and enhancing the information storage capac-ity of theoretical neural networks [15]. More funda-mentally, constraining all firing rates and pairwise co-variances may not be the most effective strategy for constructing minimal models that remain both expres-sive and computationally tractable. Identifying princi-pled alternatives—namely, which constraints can capture higher-order statistical structure without leading to an uncontrolled growth in parameters, and how to select them from data—remains an open challenge, but there is need of clarifying whether high-order interactions are not important to describe brain dynamics. In parallel, modern generative models have demon-strated remarkable capacity to encode complex, high-dimensional structure directly from data without the need to predefine explicit statistical constraints. They can generate novel samples that closely resemble the training data, with applications ranging from photo-realistic image synthesis and conversational agents to protein structure prediction. Despite their expressive power, these models typically require very large datasets and, once trained, are often difficult to interpret, mak-ing it challenging to extract explicit information about the underlying interaction structure. Shallow generative models such as Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBMs) have recently emerged as a compelling compromise be-tween traditional maximum entropy approaches and deep generative architectures. Their relative simplicity per-mits a degree of analytical investigation of the encod-ing mechanism, which is particularly valuable in a sci-entific context. RBMs have been shown to reproduce mean activities and pairwise correlations in large-scale recordings—up to 40,000 neurons simultaneously in ze-brafish larvae[16, 17]—and to reveal coarse-grained func-tional organization across neuronal groups. They have also been proposed for decoding visual stimuli as classi-fiers [18], and temporal extensions of RBMs have proven effective in modeling neural dynamics [19]. In this work we take a step further with RBMs, and we demonstrate that RBMs can yield quantitative in-sight into the underlying neural interaction network in a manner closely analogous to maximum entropy mod-els, yet without requiring the explicit specification of predefined constraints or manual structural tuning. We apply our method to model spike trains simultaneously recorded with Neuropixels probes from thousands of neu-rons across cortical and subcortical regions in mice per-forming visual tasks [5]. In contrast to standard approaches, we show that RBMs involve substantially fewer trainable parameters, leading to a more parsimonious and scalable represen-tation of large-scale neural activity, at the same time that we show that we are able to correctly reproduce high order statistics. RBMs are energy-based generative models that encode the empirical data distribution in terms of an underlying energy landscape parametrized by a bipartite neural network (Fig. 1), linking neurons and latent variables. The introduction of hidden variables makes RBMs capable of encoding all higher-order corre-lations in the data through the non-linearities in the ac-tivation function of hidden units, making them universal approximators capable of modeling arbitrarily complex data [20]. Interestingly, it has recently been shown that RBMs can be mapped onto multi-body interaction spin models, enabling the inference of effective interactions be-tween data components [21, 22] like in maximum entropy models, only that now high order couplings can also be inferred [22, 23]. A major challenge in training EBMs lies in the computational intractability of likelihood gradi-ent estimation, typically addressed through approximate methods such as Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) or 3Langevin dynamics, both of which are computationally demanding. Recent advances in MCMC-based training and sampling protocols [24] now enable training these models on datasets comprising thousands of neurons in just a few minutes and accurately estimating the log-likelihood, making RBMs particularly well suited for modeling neural activity and inferring microscopic inter-actions directly from data with minimally complex mod-els, as comparison between architectural choices can be made precisely.  

> II. DESCRIPTION OF RBMS AND MAPPING TO MULTIBODY INTERACTING MODELS

Restricted Boltzmann machines (RBMs) [25] are prob-abilistic energy-based generative models admitting a nat-ural formulation within equilibrium statistical mechanics. An RBM consists of a bipartite architecture comprising a layer of visible variables, representing observed data, and a layer of hidden variables encoding latent degrees of free-dom, with interactions restricted to inter-layer couplings. The model assigns a Boltzmann–Gibbs distribution to joint configurations of visible and hidden units, whereby hidden variables are in charge of encoding dependencies between visible nodes, and induce effective higher-order correlations between these, upon marginalization of the latent variables. Here we consider RBM architectures in which both vis-ible and hidden nodes are binary, taking values in {0, 1}.For visible units, this choice is motivated by the minimal representation of neuronal activity as a two-state process, corresponding to an active (1) or silent (0) neuron within a prescribed time window. For hidden units, this binary parametrization is known to endow RBMs with universal approximation capabilities [20]. The Hamiltonian of an RBM with binary visible units

In contrast with standard Ising-like models, where these observables correspond to single-site and pairwise correlations, the RBM induces higher-order statistical observables through the marginalization over hidden variables. The main caveat of this approach lies in the intractabil-ity of the partition function Zθ , which prevents an exact evaluation of the gradient. The data-dependent contri-bution (i.e., the first term in Eq. (5)) is straightforward to compute, as it reduces to empirical averages over the training dataset. In practice, this contribution is estimated using MCMC (MCMC) meth-ods. Recent work has emphasized the importance of con-trolling the convergence of the sampling process during training to avoid uncontrolled nonequilibrium effects and obtain good models [26, 27]. In this work, we adopt the persistent contrastive divergence scheme [28], in which a persistent Markov chain is iteratively updated as model parameters evolve. We verified that no non-monotonic effects arise when sampling the trained models for gener-ation. Moreover, all MCMC procedures used to generate the data analyzed in this study were systematically mon-itored to ensure convergence.

> III. DATA PRE-PROCESSING AND DETAILS OF TRAINING PROCEDURE A. Data pre-processing

All the data used in this work are publicly avail-able in the Allen Institute of Brain Science [5]. Each 5recording session corresponds to a unique experiment where a mouse performs a visual change-detection task, while neural activity is recorded with Neuropixels probes (specifically, firing times at single-neuron level). Each ex-perimental session has a duration of about 3 hours and comprises several stimulus blocks, including natural im-ages and Gabor stimuli (grating patches), with stimulus presentations in windows of ∼ 250ms. Each mouse is recorded on two consecutive days, one session with a fa-miliar image set (used during training) and one with a novel image set, enabling analyses of experience-related differences in neural coding. As the data contain abso-lute (real) firing times of each neuron, for our purposes a discretization is needed to extract single-time frames where each of them will account for the collective activ-ity of a population of neurons within that window. Atime-binning ∆ = 20ms is used, typically collecting only the data between the first and second hour of session. Within each time slice, all neurons which are recorded having at least one firing signal in the interval [ t, t + ∆] are assigned a +1 value, and 0 otherwise. This procedure generates a dataset of binary entries {0, 1} composed by 1hour /∆ = 3 .6 × 10 5 time frames, which are further re-duced by a factor 5 to reduce the dataset’s size. Data are then shuffled and split in a train and test set, with 0.7/0.3 proportion.  

> B. Training protocol

We initialize the RBM’s weights and hidden biases at random with small variance, while the initial visible bi-ases bi are set to bi = log ⟨σi⟩data − log (1 − ⟨ σi⟩data ), i.e., the biases an RBM with null weights would have to fit the first moments of the data. The training pro-tocol employed is the persistent contrastive divergence scheme [28] (PCD): starting from a set of initial config-urations (chains) drawn at the beginning of the train-ing, their states are updated for k steps of block Gibbs-sampling at each parameters’ update, and at every new update the last configurations at the previous update are used. Data are randomly batched in portions of 2000 samples each, and the same number of chains is used to estimate the gradients in Eq. (5).

In order to select the minimal model that captures all the complexity in the data but without overfitting, we perform model selection by looking at the log-likelihood computed on a test set along the training trajectory. As opposite to the training log-likelihood which is the quan-tity maximized by the training procedure itself, the log-likelihood on a test set indicates how well the model is able to generate data that has never seen, making it a reliable criterion to measure the model’s ability to gen-eralize beyond the training set. However, computing the log-likelihood for an RBM is non-trivial, since it requires estimating the partition function Zθ in Eq. (2), which is generally intractable and must be approximated using specialized techniques. Standard procedures involve e.g. annealed importance sampling (AIS) techniques, which however do not guarantee a reliable evaluation if not in the limit of an infinitesimal temperature annealing proto-col. In this work, we exploit the parallel trajectory tem-pering (PTT) technique introduced in [24]. In practice, as model capacity or training time increases, the training log-likelihood may continue to improve while the test log-likelihood eventually reaches a maximum and then begins to deteriorate, signaling the onset of overfitting. There-fore, the optimal model is selected at the point where the test log-likelihood is maximized, prior to the regime in which overfitting dominates.

C. Simulating neural activity 

The RBMs presented in this work are trained to re-produce the overall statistics of the data using neural responses discretized into 20 ms time bins. To construct the dataset, only one frame every 100 ms is retained, both to reduce the number of datapoints and because temporal correlations render intermediate frames largely redundant. Formally, in our approach, we treat all sam-ples as i.i.d. drawn from an unknown distribution that the model aims to approximate. However, the underlying recordings are time-dependent, and neural responses at different times exhibit temporal correlations. This raises the natural question of whether the learned models can provide insight into the internal dynamics of the system. In particular, one may ask how reliable the in silico neu-ral recordings generated through MCMC sampling are (especially when using the alternating Gibbs sampling 9procedure naturally associated with RBMs) and whether the resulting synthetic dynamics can capture aspects of the temporal organization of neural responses. Examples of real and synthetic recordings are shown in Figs. 6-(a) and ( b), which display two snapshots of experimen-tal and model-generated activity. For the experimental recordings, each time step corresponds to 20 ms of ex-perimental time. For the in silico dynamics, one time step corresponds to τ = 5 alternating Gibbs steps (i.e., five successive updates of the visible layer followed by the hidden layer). Previous studies have argued that models trained on static data are unable to reproduce the temporal statis-tics across successive time bins [30]. Here we find that, although part of the temporal structure is indeed lost—most notably the models fail to reproduce some of the oscillations observed in the autocorrelation function of the number of spikes per bin (Fig. 6( c))—the MCMC dynamics can nevertheless capture the overall relaxation of the system. In particular, when the dynamics are ini-tialized from the same configuration used to start the measurement of temporal correlations in the recording, with the solely adjustement of the effective time scale τ

is calibrated to match the experimental one, the model reproduces the relaxation from time scales as short as 20 ms (despite being trained on snapshots separated by 100 ms) up to intermediate and much longer times. Moreover, the synthetic dynamics captures several coarse-grained temporal statistics of the neural activity. In particular, it reproduces the distribution of the total number of spike counts computed over larger temporal windows, such as 100 ms or 400 ms bins (Fig. 6-( d)), as well as the conditional statistics of the population ac-tivity, measured through the average number of active neurons K(t + ∆) given the number of active neurons 

K(t) at time t. This quantity is shown in Fig. 6-( e) for several values of ∆, where the line represents the mean and the shaded region the standard deviation.  

> V. CONCLUSIONS

In this work we demonstrate that Restricted Boltz-mann Machines provide a powerful and scalable frame-work for modeling large-scale neural population activ-ity. Using Neuropixels recordings as datasets, we show that RBMs accurately reproduce the empirical statis-tics of the data while maintaining a compact and in-terpretable parametrization. In particular, the trained models match first-, second-, and higher-order statistical moments of the neural activity, as well as global observ-ables such as the distribution of population activity and the energy landscape of the inferred model. Thanks to a controlled training procedure and on-line monitoring of the log-likelihood, it is possible to select models that best describe a given dataset while minimizing overfitting. Beyond their generative capabilities, RBMs provide a principled route to extracting interpretable informa-tion about neural interactions. By mapping the trained model onto an effective multibody interaction represen-tation, we infer fields and interaction terms that reveal statistical dependencies among neurons. The resulting interaction patterns are heterogeneous and sparse, with most couplings weak and only a small subset contributing substantial interactions. Importantly, the inferred cou-plings reflect known aspects of brain organization: neu-rons in visual cortical areas exhibit stronger interactions among themselves, consistent with their coordinated ac-tivity during visually driven tasks. We can also quan-tify the importance of higher-than-two-body interactions, which shows large variations across brain areas. These results illustrate how RBMs bridge expressive generative modeling with the statistical–mechanical interpretation traditionally pursued in maximum-entropy approaches. Although the models were trained on temporally shuf-fled snapshots of neural activity, we find that the dynam-ics induced by the RBM sampling procedure reproduces several coarse-grained temporal statistics of the record-ings, including the relaxation of population activity and the distribution of spike counts over extended time win-dows. While this synthetic dynamics does not capture all temporal features—such as oscillatory structures in autocorrelation functions—it nevertheless suggests that generative models trained on static statistics can still en-code aspects of the system’s dynamical responses. Several directions for future work naturally emerge from this study. First, extending the present framework to explicitly incorporate temporal dependencies—for in-stance through temporal or recurrent variants of RBMs [19, 30, 31]—could enable a more faithful representation of neural dynamics and stimulus-driven responses. Sec-ond, the latent variables learned by the model provide a low-dimensional representation of neural population ac-tivity whose structure deserves further investigation, par-ticularly in relation to behavioral variables, stimulus fea-tures, or internal brain states. Third, comparing models trained under different behavioral conditions (such as fa-miliar versus novel stimuli) may reveal how effective in-teraction networks reorganize during learning or plastic-ity. Finally, the generative nature of the RBM framework enables controlled in silico perturbations—such as clamp-ing or modifying subsets of neurons—to probe causal re-lationships within neural populations. Taken together, these results establish Restricted Boltzmann Machines as grounded statistical-physics–inspired approach for analyzing large-scale neural recordings, and for systematic testing of pre-dictive hypotheses linking the statistical structure of the neural activity to behavioral and experimental variables. By combining scalability, generative power, and interpretability, RBMs provide a useful tool to uncover the collective structure of neural activity and to connect high-dimensional experimental data with effective models of interacting neuronal populations. 10
